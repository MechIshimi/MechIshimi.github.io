// Overview

Project Overview

Pittsburgh's hills make cycling exhausting, and e-bikes are expensive. This capstone project set out to close that gap with a purely mechanical energy recovery attachment that retrofits onto any standard bicycle. No electronics, no battery, no motor.

The system stores kinetic energy during downhill coasting by coiling a strong elastic band through a gear-driven mechanism linked to the rear wheel. A three-position lever switches between charging, neutral, and discharging modes. When the rider needs a burst of acceleration at a stop or on an uphill, a ratchet releases and the elastic uncoils, driving the rear wheel forward.

The design won Most Innovative Project at the CMU Design Expo 2024, competing against capstone teams across the full mechanical engineering department.

// My Contribution

What I Built

I was responsible for the majority of the CAD work, modeling the gear train, housing, ratchet assembly, and mounting interface in SolidWorks. Most of the individual components -- the band gear, switching gears, drum, pawl, and acrylic housing walls -- were modeled by me and iterated on as the design developed.

On the manufacturing side, I led fabrication of the physical prototype, which involved laser cutting acrylic panels, assembling the gear train, fitting bearings and bushings, and integrating the lever and ratchet mechanisms. I also owned parts procurement and budget tracking throughout the project, sourcing metric spur gears, hardware, and materials within the team's cost constraints.

// How It Works

Mechanism

Charging mode: As the bike rolls downhill, the chain link to the rear wheel drives the gear train, coiling the elastic band around the drum. A ratchet locks the drum so the energy stays stored.

Neutral mode: The switching gears disengage from the band gear. The ratchet holds the drum in place. The rider pedals freely with no drag from the system.

Discharging mode: The ratchet lifts and the switching gears re-engage with reversed directional relationship. The elastic uncoils, spinning the drum and driving the rear wheel forward. The result is a short but meaningful burst of acceleration -- useful for clearing intersections or starting from a stop on an incline.
